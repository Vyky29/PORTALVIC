# Day Centre Settling Report — Emanuel Dodson

**Period covered:** 12 June 2026 – 15 July 2026  
**Service:** Day Centre (typically Monday / Wednesday / Friday, 11:00–16:00), including Aquatic Activity  
**Support model:** **1:1** in the centre; **2:1 required for community access**

Dear Almaz,

I hope you are well.

Please find below a summary of how Emanuel has settled into the Day Centre since starting on **12 June 2026**. This report draws on session feedback recorded across his first weeks, covering engagement, the support he is receiving, and how the provision is contributing to his quality of life.

## Overview

Emanuel has made a strong and consistent start. Across **16 session records** between mid-June and mid-July, he has attended every recorded Day Centre / aquatic session, presented as **Happy/Excited**, and maintained engagement ratings of **4 or 5** throughout.

He has familiarised himself quickly with the environment, staff team, and daily structure. From his first day he accessed the gym and pool, joined group snack, and participated in shared activities (including karaoke and basketball). He has continued to arrive ready to engage and has left sessions positively, indicating a secure association with the setting and willingness to return.

His presentation aligns with a preference for predictable, movement-rich routines that combine table-based work, physical exercise, swimming, life skills, and social sport — with growing interest in short community outings when staffing allows.

## Engagement profile

Emanuel’s engagement is closely linked to access to preferred activities, clear sequencing, and opportunities for movement and choice within a structured day.

He engages well in:
- Gym-based exercise and structured circuits (with timer-supported endings)
- Swimming / aquatic sessions (push and glide, kicking, rotation, arm work, catch)
- Vocational classroom tasks (packing, matching names/envelopes, assembling product boxes)
- Maths and handwriting
- Basketball and other physical games
- Life skills at mealtimes
- End-of-day photo résumé using Colourful Semantics (who / doing what / where)
- Group social moments (karaoke, film, shared snack)
- Short community shopping trips when 2:1 support is available
- Sensory opportunities within the centre offer

Engagement remains high when:
- The day follows a familiar sequence (e.g. table work → gym → swim → lunch / life skills → basketball)
- He can request preferred activities (basketball, cake, more pool games, shopping / ice cream)
- Staff offer prompts while allowing increasing independence

His engagement pattern on most days is recorded as **Independent** or **Independent with prompts**, reflecting growing confidence within the routine.

## Daily structure and support

Emanuel is supported on a **1:1** basis within the Day Centre. This level of support is appropriate for classroom, gym, pool, and on-site activities, and enables:

- Continuous supervision across transitions (classroom, gym, pool, outdoor basketball)
- Flexible pacing between high-demand and low-demand blocks
- Immediate support around snack, medication awareness, and personal needs
- Parallel access to aquatic instruction alongside Day Centre staff

**Community access requires 2:1.** Emanuel is highly aware of his environment and of other people’s movements. Safe access to shops and other off-site activities needs a second adult so that he can leave the centre without compromising staffing for the wider group.

Staffing across this period has included Michelle, Victor, Youssef, and Raul, with aquatic sessions led by a swimming instructor. Consistency of approach has supported his settling. At present, **2:1 for community is not fixed on his timetable**; it is arranged when the staff member supporting him can be joined by the manager — most realistically on **Fridays**.

### Community access and shopping

In his first weeks we began introducing short community experiences carefully.

- **First week:** Emanuel joined a group trip to the **local supermarket** with the rest of the Day Centre group. One staff member supported him for approximately **30 minutes** of shopping alongside peers. This went well and gave him a positive first experience of community access from the setting.
- **Following week:** Having remembered that outing, Emanuel **requested to go shopping**. It was a very hot day; after sports had finished and the rest of the group were engaged in other independent activities on site, he wanted an ice cream. Staff agreed to take him out **on his own (not with the group)** and put **2:1** in place for that community trip. He went to buy an ice cream successfully.

These experiences show both his readiness for community participation and his ability to communicate preference clearly once he has a reference point for an activity.

### Awareness, circle time and choice

Emanuel is highly aware during **circle times** and group planning conversations. At these points, other participants are often offered **choice** about which activities they will access during the day — including options that staff can cover on that day (sometimes including community).

Because Emanuel does **not** currently have community activities as a fixed part of his offer, he can **see** peers selecting or accessing community options that are not routinely available to him. This is an important consideration for his emotional experience and sense of fairness. Until 2:1 community support can be planned more consistently (beyond manager-assisted Fridays), staff need to manage expectations carefully during choice-based group discussions so that he is not repeatedly exposed to options he cannot yet take up on the same basis as peers.

### Gym and physical wellbeing

Gym access has been a clear motivator and a core part of his day.

**Early weeks — long machine circuit.** At first, Emanuel wanted to try **all** the machines. He would often spend up to **around ten minutes on each**, which made the gym block very long. Even when swimming was scheduled for **12:00**, he still wanted to finish his full gym routine before moving on. This showed strong motivation for physical activity, but it also put pressure on transitions into the pool.

**Timer strategy.** Staff then introduced a **timer** within the gym routine. This worked very well. On one occasion the timer had been used successfully in another activity; the **following Wednesday**, the same approach was applied inside his gym circuit. He responded positively: he was able to **finish when the timer ended without completing every exercise**, rather than needing to run through the whole machine sequence.

By late June he had also settled into a preferred circuit structure when time allowed (e.g. treadmill, air bike, racer bike, and short crunch sets). The important development is not only the content of the workout, but his growing ability to **accept a time boundary** and move on.

Gym work continues to support strength, cardiovascular fitness, motor planning, and a sense of achievement. Basketball in the afternoon further extends physical activity and social participation.

### Flexibility and changes of routine

One of the priorities mum asked us to explore was how Emanuel would respond when a planned activity was **changed or shortened** — for example, cutting swimming on a given day.

He is showing clear progress in this area. When a change is needed, staff offer **alternative preferred activities** that we know he enjoys. He has been responding **very well**, without becoming angry or distressed. The timer work in the gym is one concrete example of this improvement in transitions; the same pattern is seen when swimming or other blocks are adjusted. This is an important quality-of-life gain: greater flexibility reduces conflict and makes the day more sustainable for him and for the team.

### Aquatic activity

Swimming has become a highlight. Feedback describes highly engaged pool sessions, including days where he requested **more activities** when offered breaks. Skills practised include push and glide, kicking, rotation, arm movement, and catch games. Aquatic work contributes not only to water confidence and fitness, but also to emotional regulation, turn-taking, and following multi-step instructions in a sensory-rich environment. Where swimming is shortened or replaced, preferred alternatives are offered so the change remains manageable.

### Classroom, life skills and leisure

Within the centre Emanuel accesses a mix of **sensory**, **vocational**, academic and leisure activities.

**Vocational / adult-oriented motor skills.** A significant part of his classroom offer is vocational work designed to build skills that could later transfer to roles such as a **stock assistant / reponedor**. These are adult-oriented tasks rather than children’s sorting games: for example matching names and packing cards into envelopes, assembling boxes of different product items, and other packing / matching sequences. He manages these activities well and engages appropriately with the demand.

**Academic work.** He does **maths** and **handwriting**. He does **not** typically do puzzles as part of his offer.

**Sensory and leisure.** He also accesses sensory opportunities and shared leisure (cake, movie, karaoke) alongside lunchtime / life-skills routines at the table.

**End-of-day résumé.** At the end of the day the group completes a **résumé of the day**, reviewing and celebrating photos of where each person has been — including photos of Emanuel and of his peers. Emanuel comments on the photos using **Colourful Semantics**, naming **who** is doing **what**, and often **where** (location). This supports communication, social attention, and shared celebration of the day.

These opportunities support independence, functional adult-oriented skills, communication, and social belonging — all central to quality of life.

## Communication and choice

Emanuel communicates verbally and is able to express preferences clearly (e.g. requesting badminton, accepting basketball as an alternative; asking for cake; requesting shopping / ice cream after learning that community trips were possible; choosing to continue to swimming even when momentarily uncertain). Staff use calm language, visual structure where helpful, and responsive choice-making so that he retains control within a predictable framework — while being mindful that offering community choices he cannot yet access regularly can increase frustration given how aware he is of peers’ options.

## Health, regulation and family liaison

- On his first day, medication was left by family and stored securely on site, with arrangements to move to the locker system and medication recording from the following week.
- **Snack, fluid and “vomit” routine.** After eating, Emanuel often follows a familiar sequence that can include wanting to vomit. He typically says **“vomit”** (rather than a euphemism). We do **not** assume this is purely behavioural: it may also relate to physical discomfort (for example throat discomfort), but what we observe day to day is that it functions as part of his **routine**. Staff control water intake during and after snack where possible, because without enough liquid in the stomach it is harder for him to complete that sequence. Results vary: sometimes the strategy reduces the behaviour; on other days he still finds a way to drink more — including helping himself at the tap / sink, or saving a last sip from his glass — in order to finish the routine. There are also times when he has asked about vomiting **outside** post-meal times. Importantly, when given a clear **choice** between going to vomit and doing a preferred activity (for example basketball), he has **chosen the activity and not gone to vomit**. This suggests he can, at least sometimes, **control when he does and does not** follow through. The current strategy when he says **“vomit”** at the end of the meal is to replace the usual post-meal **relaxation** block with a **preferred, motivating activity**, and for staff to use the **visual schedule** during the transition of clearing the table — **before** he drinks the glass of water — to interrupt the sequence. So far this has been tried a small number of times; he has still taken the glass / saved a sip to complete the routine. The team will continue working on this approach in partnership with family.
- Related to flexibility more broadly, he has at times verbalised “vomit” before changing for swimming and then **chosen to continue** to the pool after a calm check-in.
- On 13 July, mum shared that he had tonsillitis and provided throat spray. He declined the spray during the day but maintained good energy and completed his session.
- Hand-over to mum has been positive, including confirmation of calm mornings and good days.

Daily updates and photos remain an important bridge between the centre and home, supporting continuity of care and shared celebration of progress.

## Quality of life impact

Since starting, Emanuel has gained:
- A stable weekday routine with meaningful physical, aquatic, cognitive and social activity
- Increased independence within a supported **1:1** structure on site
- Early, successful community experiences (group supermarket; individual ice-cream outing with 2:1)
- Clear progress with **transitions and changes of routine** (timer in the gym; accepting shortened or replaced swimming when preferred alternatives are offered — an area mum specifically wanted us to trial)
- Strong positive emotional association with the setting
- Opportunities to practise communication, choice, and perseverance
- Improved access to fitness and swimming as regular parts of his week

Taken together, these elements support wellbeing, self-confidence, and growing access to community-based opportunities when staffing allows.

## Summary and recommendations

Emanuel has settled exceptionally well. Session data shows consistent attendance, high engagement, and a happy presentation across the review period. The current Day Centre model — combining structured table work, gym, swimming, life skills and sport — matches his motivators and maintains regulation on a **1:1** basis.

Community access is a clear next step for quality of life, but it **requires 2:1**. Early supermarket and ice-cream outings went well; he now actively requests this type of activity. The main constraint is staffing: without a fixed second adult, community trips are currently limited to times when his allocated staff can be supported by the **manager (typically Fridays)**. Because he is highly aware during group choice / circle time, seeing peers access community options he cannot regularly join is a live issue that needs careful management.

We recommend continuing:
1. **1:1 support** as the standard model within the centre  
2. **2:1 for community** whenever he leaves the site (shops, ice cream, local outings)  
3. Exploring a more **predictable 2:1 community slot** (beyond ad hoc Friday manager cover) so community can sit fairly within his weekly offer  
4. Careful handling of **circle-time / group choice** so community options are not repeatedly presented when 2:1 is not available that day  
5. **Timer-supported gym sessions** and continued practice of flexible endings / activity changes with preferred alternatives  
6. Ongoing **snack / “vomit” routine work**: controlled fluids, preferred post-meal activities instead of immediate relaxation, schedule prompts before the water glass; continue trial-and-review with family  
7. The established **gym + aquatic + vocational classroom** focus as core weekly pillars  
8. Continued **photo / verbal handovers** and end-of-day résumé with Colourful Semantics  

Emanuel has clear potential to continue progressing within this provision when on-site support remains 1:1 and community access is planned with reliable 2:1 cover.
