This report brings together observations from his May sessions and his more recent block of attendance from late June into July, with a focus on engagement, the support he is receiving, and how the provision contributes to his quality of life.

## Overview

Timi first accessed Day Centre sessions in **May 2026** (including 11, 15 and 18 May). After a gap — including a cancelled session on 22 May due to traffic — he returned on **29 June 2026** and has since attended a further set of sessions through mid-July.

Across recorded feedback, his presentation is most often **Happy/Excited**, with several sessions rated at the highest engagement level (**5**). He has shown that, when the day is predictable and sensory needs are met early, he can follow the centre routine, engage in table work, access the pool, enjoy snack, and leave calmly.

His settling has not been linear: some days are outstanding, while others require more intensive support around transitions, toileting when highly excited, and variable willingness to enter the pool. These fluctuations are expected as he continues to build trust in a structured setting, and they guide how we tailor support rather than indicating that the placement is not working.

## Engagement profile

Timi’s engagement is closely linked to:
- Familiar sequencing and reduced waiting
- Early access to preferred / regulating spaces (especially the sensory room)
- Predictable transitions into the pool and changing routine
- Calm, consistent adult support (2:1)
- Positive reinforcement through preferred snack and shared celebration of success

He engages well in:
- Sensory room time as a settling / regulating start
- Table-top and fine-motor activities (puzzles, sorting, matching, magnets, buttons)
- Pool sessions on days when he is ready (object retrieval games, jumping for toys, full water participation)
- Snack after activity blocks
- Clear arrival / leaving routines (backpack, belongings, goodbye to carers)

Engagement decreases when:
- Preferred spaces are not immediately available on arrival
- Transitions feel rushed or uncertain (particularly pool / changing)
- Sensory or emotional load rises (excitement or anxiety)
- Demands outpace regulation

Behaviours at these times are understood as communicative: signalling a need for predictability, sensory regulation, personal care support, or more time.

## May introductory period

In May, Timi attended shorter afternoon sessions and began learning the environment.

- On **15 May**, he participated in lots of activities with high engagement, presenting happily. Staff also noted a toileting accident linked to excitement while moving in front of a screen; carers were informed and he was changed promptly.
- On **18 May**, he arrived happy but was unsettled for around **30 minutes** while waiting for the sensory room. Once access was available he used trampoline briefly and table work, then returned to sensory space. Anxiety reduced after that settling period.

These early sessions confirmed the importance of **immediate sensory access on arrival**, low-demand starts, and close personal-care awareness when he is highly stimulated.

## Return and settling since 29 June 2026

### Strong settling days

**29 June** marked a clear positive shift. Timi settled well with carers, completed table work (puzzles and motor skills), and had a **very good pool session**, including getting into the water — a notable step compared with the previous Friday. After swimming he enjoyed fries and crisps. Carers commented on how calm and participating he was. Engagement was rated **5**, with independence supported by prompts, and photos shared at handover.

**3 July** was described as another **spectacular** session. He arrived and immediately followed the routine (backpack away, goodbye to carers, sensory room). Despite a toileting accident linked to excitement, he was changed quickly and returned to activities. He then swam **calmly and fully**, enjoyed snack, and left without difficulty. Photos and videos were shared with family (including while mum was on holiday), supporting continuity and trust.

These days demonstrate Timi’s capacity to access a full, meaningful short Day Centre block when regulation and support are well matched.

### Days requiring higher support

**6 July** — Timi arrived happy but also anxious. The session followed the expected structure (sensory → table work → pool transition). He showed resistance entering the pool area, then agreed to the changing room; after showering he had brief positive interactions with noodles in the water. Engagement was lower (**3**) and he required **full support**. Despite this, handover with mum and carers was positive, and mum expressed happiness with his progress overall.

**10 July** — Engagement remained good overall (**4**), with a mix of independence with prompts and regular support. Staff noted recurring toileting when he is highly excited / sensory-seeking on the floor. A clear strategy was agreed: on arrival, change into a **nappy for the centre period** so that accidents can be managed without interrupting the day, followed by showering and the same supportive approach around the pool. Carers are asked to ensure towel, flip-flops and underpants are available.

**13 July** — Engagement was recorded as low (**1**) with full support required. Written detail for that day is limited; it sits within a pattern of variable pool readiness and high support needs on some transitions. We continue to treat these days as information for refining the approach rather than as setbacks to his overall settling.

## Aquatic activity and water confidence

Swimming is a central opportunity for Timi’s physical wellbeing, sensory regulation, and confidence.

On his strongest days he:
- Enters the water and participates fully
- Practises functional pool games (throwing / retrieving objects; jumping to collect toys into a bag — repeated successfully)
- Remains calm enough for staff to extend the activity

On more challenging days he may resist the pool environment or changing sequence, try to remove swimwear, or need extended preparation. Staff respond with patience, reduced demands, and step-by-step support rather than pressure. The goal remains **positive association with water**, not forced performance.

Aquatic sessions are supervised with autism-informed support alongside the Day Centre team, consistent with his **2:1** requirement.

## Communication, routine and independence

Timi is non-verbal and benefits from PECS / visuals, familiar staff interpretation, and low language. Within sessions we support:
- Following a known sequence (arrival → sensory → table work → pool → snack → leave)
- Placing belongings and greeting / goodbye routines
- Choice and participation through modelling and physical / visual prompts

Gains in these micro-routines are meaningful for daily life: they reduce anxiety, increase predictability, and build a sense of belonging in a community setting.

## Family communication and partnership

We maintain close liaison with carers and mum, including photo/video sharing after strong sessions and open discussion of strategies (toileting support, pool transitions, what to pack). Family feedback has been encouraging, including explicit happiness with progress even on days that required more support.

Parent-provided food remains in place as agreed. Sessions stay **on-site** in line with the request that Timi is not taken off premises.

## Support model (2:1) and quality of life

Timi requires **2:1 support when regulated**, with the capacity for two adults to respond when distressed. In the Day Centre this enables:

- One adult to lead the activity while another manages transitions, personal care, or environmental load  
- Safe access to sensory spaces, classroom tasks and the pool within a short, intensive block  
- Immediate response to toileting needs without shaming or prolonged disruption  
- Protection in environments where noise or peer distress could escalate him  

Without this level of support, his ability to remain regulated, access swimming, and complete the session meaningfully would be significantly limited.

### Quality of life outcomes we are seeing

- Growing familiarity and positive association with the Day Centre routine  
- Access to sensory regulation as a planned part of the day  
- Meaningful physical activity through swimming on ready days  
- Moments of calm participation that carers have celebrated  
- Shared visual updates that keep family connected to his week  
- A structured community day opportunity that supports wellbeing beyond home  

## Summary and recommendations

Timi is settling into the Day Centre with clear evidence of success, particularly on days when sensory settling comes first and transitions are well paced. His strongest sessions show high engagement, full pool participation, enjoyable snack, and positive family handover. Variability around toileting when excited and pool entry is expected and is being addressed through agreed practical strategies.

We recommend continuing:
1. **2:1 staffing** for all Day Centre / aquatic blocks  
2. **Sensory-first arrival** to reduce early unsettled periods  
3. The agreed **nappy / change strategy** during sessions, with carers packing towel, flip-flops and underpants  
4. **Low-pressure aquatic access** — celebrate entry and participation; never force  
5. Predictable short timetable (**~11:00–13:00**) with table work + pool + snack  
6. Ongoing **photo / video updates** and joint review of strategies with family  

Timi has demonstrated that, with the right structure and support, he can be calm, engaged and successful in this setting. Continued partnership between home and the Day Centre will be essential to consolidate these gains and further improve his quality of life.
