This combined report summarises how Emanuel has settled into clubSENsational Day Centre since 12 June 2026. It outlines the activities and outcomes observed by the Day Centre team and their alignment with advice from Tri-Borough Occupational Therapy and Speech and Language Therapy following the July 2026 visit.

It also explains why funded 2:1 support for defined community hours is required for him to access his preferred places in the community on a fair and predictable basis.

1. Overview: settling and presentation

Overall picture Emanuel has made a strong and consistent start. He has familiarised himself quickly with the environment, staff team and daily structure. From day one, he accessed the gym and pool, joined the group for a snack and took part in shared activities. He arrives ready to engage and finishes each day positively, showing a secure association with the setting.

2. Daily structure and on-site support (1:1)

Emanuel is supported on a 1:1 basis within the Day Centre. This model fits classroom, gym, pool and other on-site activities, and gives staff enough flexibility to follow his motivators while keeping the day predictable.

He attends on Monday, Wednesday and Friday from 11:00 to 16:00. Engagement is strongest when the day follows a clear sequence with movement, choice and familiar transitions. A typical flow is table work and Hub time, then gym, then aquatic activity, then a return to the Hub for sports, life skills and, when staffing allows, community access. Hub means the Day Centre base: classroom, circle time, life skills and social activity between the more physical blocks of the day.

On site, the 1:1 worker stays with him across transitions, adjusts the pace between higher- and lower-demand activities, supports snacks, medication awareness and personal needs, and links him into aquatic instruction with Day Centre cover. Staffing across the period has included Michelle, Victor, Youssef and Raul, with swimming sessions led by an instructor. Consistency of approach has supported settling, and Tri-Borough SLT notes that behaviour is more settled with a familiar staff team.

Physical, aquatic and regulation

  • Gym circuits with timer-supported endings
  • Aquatic work: breathing control, body position through push and glide, kicking, coordination, rotation, arm movement and structured aquatic games
  • Sports and motor coordination: basketball, football, badminton and related games
  • Proprioceptive and vestibular input through movement, resistance and balance tasks
  • Interoception and regulation practice, including calm check-ins and body-awareness prompts
  • Short mindfulness and settling activities when he needs to reset between blocks

Classroom, life skills and social

  • Circle time and shared group planning with visual choice supports
  • Vocational packing, matching and assembling (stock-assistant style)
  • Fine motor work: handwriting, word search, sorting, packing and table-top sequences
  • Maths and functional classroom tasks
  • Life skills at mealtimes; group snack, karaoke and film
  • End-of-day photo review with Colourful Semantics
  • Supported community access when 2:1 is available

Within that offer he can choose preferred sports, request more pool or gym time when appropriate, take part in circle time, practise fine motor and vocational tasks, and use regulating activities that support proprioception, interoception and emotional settling. Engagement stays high when the sequence is familiar, when he can ask for preferred activities, and when staff give prompts while still leaving room for independence. Most days are recorded as Independent or Independent with prompts.

Flexibility when plans change

Emanuel’s mother asked the team to explore how he would respond when a planned activity was changed or shortened, for example by shortening a swimming session. He is showing clear progress. When a change is needed, staff offer alternative preferred activities, and he has responded well without becoming angry or dysregulated. The gym timer is one concrete example. The same pattern appears when swimming or other activity blocks are adjusted. This represents important progress in his development. Greater flexibility reduces conflict and makes the day more sustainable.

3. Gym and physical wellbeing

Gym access has been a clear motivator. In the early weeks Emanuel wanted to try all the machines (often around ten minutes on each), which made the gym block very long and put pressure on the 12:00 swim transition. Staff then introduced a timer within the gym routine. He responded positively: he could finish when the timer ended without completing every exercise. By late June, he had settled into a preferred circuit when time allowed, including the treadmill, air bike, racer bike and short crunch sets. Gym work supports strength, fitness, motor planning and achievement; afternoon sport extends physical and social participation.

4. Aquatic activity

Swimming has become one of the highlights of Emanuel’s weekly programme. He engages positively in pool sessions and remains motivated during both structured instruction and play-based activities. Session feedback records a high level of engagement, including occasions when he requested additional activities rather than taking an offered break. He practises breathing control, body position through push and glide, kicking, coordination, rotation, arm movement and structured aquatic games, with individual guidance helping him build confidence and maintain participation.

The aquatic programme supports water confidence, fitness, coordination, emotional regulation, communication and turn-taking. It also gives Emanuel regular opportunities to make choices, follow short multi-step instructions and sustain engagement within a predictable, sensory-rich environment, in line with OT advice on proprioceptive and vestibular input.

5. Classroom, vocational skills and communication

Classroom time is a central part of Emanuel’s Day Centre offer. A large share of it is vocational and adult-oriented, designed to build practical routines that could later transfer to roles such as stock assistant. He works on matching names and placing cards into envelopes, assembling product boxes, packing and completing multi-step matching sequences. Alongside this he practises maths, handwriting and other fine-motor table tasks that strengthen attention, accuracy and perseverance.

He also takes part in circle time and shared group planning, where visual supports help him follow the sequence of the day and make choices. This classroom structure gives him repeated opportunities to practise turn-taking, waiting, commenting and negotiating preferred activities with familiar staff.

Emanuel has particularly benefited from personalised digital visual aids that clubSENsational created for him. These show his avatar completing the Day Centre routine and the different places where he takes part in activities, including classroom, gym, pool and Hub spaces. Introducing this digitally has made the daily sequence clearer and more motivating for him, supported smoother transitions, and strengthened his understanding of what comes next. The team will continue to implement and develop this approach as part of his ongoing support.

At the end of the day, the group completes a day review using photos of where each person has been. Emanuel uses Colourful Semantics (who, doing what and where) to comment, which supports communication, social attention and shared celebration of the day. He communicates verbally and expresses preferences clearly: requesting a preferred sport, accepting an alternative when needed, choosing a snack, asking for community activities, and deciding to continue with swimming after a calm check-in.

6. Community access: why 2:1 is essential

Support request 1:1 is appropriate on site. Community access requires 2:1. Without a second adult for defined hours each week, clubSENsational cannot safely or fairly offer Emanuel regular access to his preferred community venues, even though he is thriving in the building-based programme and has already shown readiness for community participation.

What has already worked

  • First week: Emanuel joined a group supermarket trip with peers. One staff member supported him for about 30 minutes of shopping, providing a positive first community experience.
  • Following week: having remembered that outing, he requested another community activity. Staff put 2:1 in place for an individual local outing, which he completed successfully.

These experiences show both readiness for community participation and clear communication of his preferences once he has a reference point for an activity.

Current constraint

At present, 2:1 for community is not fixed on his timetable. It is arranged when his allocated staff member can be joined by the manager, most realistically on Fridays. Emanuel is highly aware of his environment and of other people’s movements. Safe access to shops and other off-site activities requires a second adult so he can leave the centre without compromising staffing for the wider group, and so one worker can support him closely while the other manages logistics, monitors the environment and provides additional support.

Fairness and circle time

During circle times and group planning, peers are often offered a choice of activities, sometimes including community options staff can cover that day. Because community is not yet a fixed part of Emanuel’s offer, he can see peers selecting options that are not routinely available to him. Given how aware he is, this affects his emotional experience and sense of fairness. Until 2:1 community support can be planned more consistently (beyond manager-assisted Fridays), staff must manage expectations carefully so he is not repeatedly exposed to options he cannot take up on the same basis as peers.

Impact if 2:1 community hours are not funded He would continue to do well in the Day Centre building, but access to preferred community venues (shopping centres, supermarkets, parks, museums and other places in the local area chosen according to his interests) could not be offered regularly. That limits choice, reduces opportunities for regulating activity in the places he enjoys, and leaves an avoidable fairness gap with peers who already access community when staffing allows.

7. Health, regulation and family liaison

  • Medication was provided by the family on day one and stored securely. A locker system and recording process were introduced the following week.
  • Post-meal “vomit” routine: after eating, he often follows a familiar sequence and typically says “vomit”. Staff do not treat this as purely behavioural. It may also relate to physical discomfort, although it functions as part of his routine.
  • Support strategies: staff manage fluid intake where possible, offer a preferred motivating activity instead of immediate post-meal relaxation, and use the visual schedule while clearing the table and before offering a glass of water.
  • Choice and engagement: when offered a preferred activity as an alternative to continuing the familiar post-meal routine, he has sometimes chosen the activity. Work continues in partnership with his family.
  • On 13 July, his mother reported that he had tonsillitis and provided throat spray. He declined the spray but maintained good energy and completed the session.
  • Handovers to his mother have been positive. Daily updates and photos remain an important bridge between the centre and home.

8. Agreed approach with Tri-Borough OT and SLT

Following their visits and joint discussions with the OT and SLT teams, clubSENsational will continue to follow the agreed support approach. This includes familiar and consistent staffing, visual timetables and countdowns, short and clear language, structured activity blocks, regular sensory and movement opportunities, and visual support for transitions, communication and choice. This includes the personalised digital visual aids created with Emanuel’s avatar to represent his Day Centre routine and the places where he takes part in activities.

9. Progress and developmental impact

Since starting, Emanuel has gained:

  • A stable weekday routine with meaningful physical, aquatic, cognitive and social activity
  • Increased independence with 1:1 support on site
  • Early successful community experiences, including a group supermarket trip and access to parks and sports pitches with 2:1 support
  • Clear progress with transitions and changes of routine, including use of the gym timer and acceptance of shortened or replaced swimming sessions with preferred alternatives
  • Strong positive emotional association with the setting
  • Opportunities to practise communication, choice and perseverance, develop vocational classroom skills and use Colourful Semantics during the end-of-day review
  • Clear benefit from personalised digital visual aids using his avatar to show the Day Centre routine and activity locations
  • Regular fitness and swimming as pillars of his week

10. Summary

Emanuel has settled exceptionally well. Session data shows consistent attendance, high engagement and a happy presentation. The Day Centre model of structured table work, gym, swimming, life skills and sport matches his motivators and maintains regulation on a 1:1 basis on site.

Community access is the clear next step to open up further opportunities for his development, independence and social participation, but it requires 2:1. Early outings went well; he now actively requests this type of activity. The main constraint is staffing: without a fixed second adult, trips are limited to manager-assisted cover (typically Fridays). Because he is highly aware during group choice, seeing peers access community options that he cannot regularly join could create an avoidable and potentially unfair disparity in the opportunities available to him.

We recommend
  1. Continue 1:1 as the standard model within the centre
  2. Fund and plan 2:1 for community whenever he leaves the site, including shopping centres, supermarkets, parks, museums and other places in the local area that he chooses
  3. Establish a predictable 2:1 community slot (beyond ad hoc Friday manager cover) so community sits fairly in his weekly offer
  4. Handle circle-time and group-choice activities carefully so community options are not repeatedly presented when 2:1 is unavailable
  5. Continue timer-supported gym sessions and flexible endings with preferred alternatives
  6. Continue work with his family on the post-meal routine in which he says “vomit”, including fluid management, preferred post-meal activities and visual schedule prompts
  7. Keep gym, aquatic and vocational classroom activities as core elements of his weekly programme
  8. Continue photo and verbal handovers, together with the end-of-day Colourful Semantics review
  9. Continue developing and using personalised digital visual aids with his avatar to support routine, transitions and place recognition across the centre

Emanuel has clear potential to keep progressing when on-site support remains 1:1 and community access is planned with reliable 2:1 cover. This would give him the same opportunities as other clients to access his preferred places in the community.
